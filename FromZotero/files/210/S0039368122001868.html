Abstract

While the pursuitworthiness of philosophical ideas has changed over time, philosophical practice and methodology have not kept pace. The worthiness of a philosophical pursuit includes not only the ideas and objectives one pursues but also the methods with which one pursues them. In this paper, we articulate how empirical approaches benefit philosophy of science, particularly advocating for the use of qualitative methods for understanding the social and normative aspects of scientific inquiry. After situating qualitative methods within empirical philosophy of science, we discuss how to adapt these traditionally sociological methods to empirically inform philosophical questions. Our aim is to normalize and legitimize qualitative methods for philosophical purposes and discuss how they can elucidate descriptive and normative components of scientific practice in a more generalizable non-idealized manner. We contend that qualitative methods are particularly well suited to philosophical interest in the social norms of science, their achievability, and their mutability. Furthermore, unlike more historical case studies in philosophy, qualitative methods enable more confidence in generalizability, albeit limited, from a concrete sample to a larger class. We conclude by addressing anxieties about the distinctness of empirical philosophy of science from social epistemology and from sociology of science.

1. Introduction

Philosophers have spent ample time deliberating over criteria for judging whether scientific proposals are worthy of further inquiry (Šešelja, Kosolosky, & Strasser, 2012; Shaw, 2022). So, let's turn this question of “pursuitworthiness” back on philosophy itself: When considering the worthiness of a pursuit in philosophy, the issue of “worthy” methodology is both powerful and overlooked. In the past, philosophers of science have often defined their own discipline as chiefly theoretical in approach, in opposition to other disciplines that have more empirical methodologies. A classic example involves the distinction between the context of discovery versus the context of justification. With the discovery-justification dichotomy, Hans Reichenbach (1938) intended to separate the empirical questions about scientists' actual reasoning for the psychologists and sociologists from the rational reconstructions of their idealized reasoning for philosophers (see Schickore, 2008). The Reichenbachian approach to limiting how philosophers of science pursue their research (i.e., through rational reconstructions) is an example of what sociologists call “boundary-work,” where practitioners of some discipline seek to establish their credibility and authority by defining essential features of their approaches and demarcating “good” from “bad” research (Gieryn, 1999). While boundary-work is unavoidable to some extent, anxieties about disciplinary traditions are not good reasons alone to exclude useful methodologies. In this paper, we articulate how empirical approaches, specifically qualitative methods, benefit philosophy of science for understanding the social and normative aspects of scientific inquiry and practice.

As a self-reflective contribution to this special issue on pursuitworthiness, we argue against opposing philosophy to sociology and in support of broadening the methodological scope of philosophy of science. Reichenbach's specific program has long been abandoned, given the interactive process between discovery and justification (Schickore & Steinle, 2006). Nonetheless, there is similar boundary-work in philosophy of science today that either excludes some empirical methods (apart from regular practices of historical case studies) or relegates them to the margins. In contrast, we argue that qualitative methods specifically are worthy of pursuit to empirically inform certain philosophical questions about scientific inquiry in practice. We demonstrate the importance of studying how reliable knowledge is generated by looking not only at published papers but also by collecting and analyzing qualitative data about social practices of reasoning and collaborating in science.

Today, it is uncontroversial for philosophers of science to embed themselves in scientific communities (e.g., Tuana, 2013); what is questioned in our discipline is whether sociological methods belong within philosophy proper, echoing Reichenbach. For example, in Conceptual Analysis for Nanoscience, Bursten et al. (2016) state that “philosophy of science investigates the nature of scientific reasoning and the implications of scientific theories for both understanding the natural world and acting in it. … Answering these questions is not a matter of collecting data nor of interpreting the results of individual experiments. Rather, it is a matter of conceptual analysis: evaluating the implications of understanding a scientific concept in one way rather than another” (2016, p. 1917, emphasis added). Philosophers generally accept some empirical modes of inquiry as complementary to conceptual analysis, such as case studies from history and social science and agent-based modeling. Yet qualitative methods like ethnology, qualitative interviews, and thematic coding remain marginal. We challenge such marginalization by arguing that analyzing qualitative data about scientific inquiry is precisely the missing link that this empirically oriented philosophy can provide to address the gap between philosophical theorizing and the actual practice of scientific inquiry.

The skeptical philosopher might ask: why should philosophers take up sociological methods? If we do, how would philosophical practice differ from sociology? In this paper, we articulate how to adapt sociological qualitative methods for philosophical purposes, and we contend that qualitative methods are particularly well suited to philosophical interest in the social norms of science, their achievability, and their mutability. Thus, we lend support to existing empirical philosophy of science, including the benefits afforded by qualitative methods (Wagenknecht, 2016; Wagenknecht, Nersessian, & Andersen, 2015). They enable us to study the reasoning processes of science from the angle of working scientists, including interpersonal interactions, the context of practice, and collective processes contributing to knowledge generation. Along these lines, Helen Longino (2002, p. 205) has argued that rationality and sociality are not opposed but rather integrated aspects of the science process: “Establishing what the data are, what the descriptive categories and their boundaries are, what counts as acceptable reasoning, which assumptions are legitimate and which not becomes a matter of social interactions as much as matter of interaction with the material world.” Any individual scientific act is part of a larger communal process, and qualitative methods are well posed to capture such interplay of “individuals without individualism” (Longino, 2002, p. 145) by emphasizing epistemic relationality.

The significance of this paper is to motivate more disciplinary reflection and openness to adapting methods from other disciplines for philosophical purposes: We address our fellow philosophers of science to better establish qualitative methods as legitimate philosophical practice instead of drawing sharp, rigid boundaries along the use of such methods as “non-philosophical.” Our case for qualitative methodologies in philosophy of science is advocacy for a more inclusive and diverse approach to boundary-work in philosophy. Rather than sharply distinguishing “philosophical” practices from “sociological” ones, we suggest drawing wider, more encompassing, and more flexible boundaries. Whether it be about demarcating science from pseudo-science or empirical methodology from philosophical methodology, all boundary-work is engrossed in political skirmishes over epistemic credibility, authority, funding, as well as social legitimacy (Gieryn, 1999). Our support motivates not only opening up and normalizing such practices in research but also changing the curriculum of graduate programs to actively offer more training and courses in methodology. For instance, while graduate programs often require courses in logic, we suggest expanding the options offered to students to include other “tool skills,” such as ethnography, interviewing, computer programming/modeling, and statistics.1

To set the stage, we begin our discussion in Section 2 by showcasing how the different fields contribute to empirical philosophy of science, including social epistemology, sociology of science, and mainstream philosophy of science. Section 3 describes how to use and adapt qualitative methods to answer philosophical research questions. We focus on how processes of social reasoning at interpersonal and collective levels contribute to generating scientific knowledge. To show how philosophy of science would benefit from integrating qualitative methods, Section 4 explains how they relate to the traditional philosophical aims of normativity and generalizability. Finally, Section 5 returns to the issue of boundary-work in philosophy to contest the remaining disciplinary barriers for pursuing philosophy of science empirically.

2. Positioning qualitative methods in empirical philosophy of science

To explain where we are going, we first describe where we currently stand. As previously described by Wagenknecht et al. (2015), empirical philosophy of science grew from the interdisciplinary field of integrated history and philosophy of science (&HPS), lying somewhere between philosophy, history, and sociology. A bit more specifically, we locate qualitative empirical philosophy of science as situated between three interrelated fields: philosophy of science, social epistemology, and sociology of science. We begin by describing these three disciplinary roots of qualitative empirical philosophy of science in its current incarnation, and then, building on this foundation, we provide an argument that this approach is particularly well suited to answering philosophical questions about the social and normative dimensions of science. While drawing on sociology for its empirical methods, a specifically qualitative empirical approach is rooted in philosophy for its research questions and conceptual background. It offers significant potential to philosophers to study processes and factors involved in scientific reasoning, such as values, norms, and justifications, through analyzing scientists’ descriptions of their reasoning as self-reflective and relational within their day-to-day practice.

2.1. Philosophy of science & scientific practice

From the more general field of philosophy of science, this empirical offshoot draws its disciplinary foundation for both classic and contemporary questions, as well as a canon and the methodology of historical case studies. Since the so-called “historical turn,” philosophers of science have increasingly looked to psychology and history for more empirically grounded accounts of science, resulting in interdisciplinary &HPS (Mauskopf & Schmaltz, 2012; Rheinberger, 2010; Schickore, 2011a; Thorén, 2015). As historical case studies became more common in debates over theory change, some philosophers bemoaned the “marriage of convenience” between history and philosophy (Giere, 1973). Others defended the arrangement from a more “historicist” bent for checking the excesses of pure “logicism” with the actual record of practice (McMullin, 1974). Within &HPS, philosophers like Larry Laudan aimed to develop theories of science to be tested against historical data, only to find that confronting a philosophical theory with historical evidence was as indecisive as empirical evidence more generally. In contrast to definitively testing theories against historical evidence, such evidence affords philosophers the hermeneutic ability to trace conceptual change via interpretation of the past with provisional concepts (Schickore, 2011b)—one of the starting points for qualitative methods in philosophy of science.

Limitations notwithstanding, philosophers of science over the past two decades have taken an “empirical turn” or “practice turn” in their work to better understand the actual practice of science (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016; Rouse, 2002; Soler, Trizio, Nickles, & Wimsatt, 2012; Soler, Zwart, Lynch, & Israel-Jost, 2014). Based on the discovery/justification dichotomy, philosophers of science have often assumed that arguments stated in publications represented a sufficiently accurate picture of scientific theorizing and thus underestimated the challenges arising in the acting and doing of science (Schickore, 2008). With the shift to thinking about science as practice, philosophers of science have begun to look more closely into experimentation (Feest & Steinle, 2015), collaboration (Ankeny & Leonelli, 2016; Kukla, 2012), consensus building (Solomon, 2015), measurement (Chang, 2007; Tal, 2017), replication (Hangel & Schickore, 2017), and other non-theoretical components of science. With qualitative methods empirical philosophy of science can draw on the social level of analysis (e.g., interpersonal, collaborative, exchange of communities) and can attend to the question of how reliable knowledge is generated interpersonally and collectively.

But what exactly is “scientific practice”? Practice is a versatile term within philosophy, its conceptual history starts long before the 1990's when theorizing itself became recognized as a social practice next to the obvious practice of experimentation (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016). Although different aspects of scientific practice, such as experimental practice, discursive theorizing and cognizing, and heuristic of practice, are distinct from each other, they all contribute to and are constitutive of generating scientific theories, hypotheses, and models. Furthermore, knowledge practices depend on communal norms. Knowledge generation is supported by virtues and shared norms–both epistemic norms and social norms of rules governing each activity of the process, which might differ between groups and fields (see Hicks & Stapleford, 2016, p. 454). Strategies derived from previous experiences help to address similar problems and to assess what questions are worthy of pursuit and the forms of evidence needed to evaluate an experimental outcome; moreover, researchers use practices of cognizing to determine how the interpersonal exchange between collaborating researchers is carried out to reach a shared goal (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016, p. 542). As Longino (1990) famously wrote, “science is social knowledge”; accordingly, scientific practice includes normative aspects of knowledge generation as well as social aspects. We will deepen this thought in Section 4.1.

2.2. Social epistemology & interpersonal processes

Like philosophers of science, epistemologists over the last three decades have begun to acknowledge the role for social perspectives on knowledge in respect to interpersonal, collective, and institutional epistemology (Goldman, 2020). Social epistemology contrasts with more traditional, individualistically oriented approaches in how they understand processes of belief formation and knowledge generation. When looking at empirical knowledge and interpersonal scientific practice, social epistemologists contended that the focus on individual belief formation is not particularly helpful because true justified belief of a person is not sufficient for claiming reliable factual truth (Goldman, 1999; see also Gettier, 1963). Interpersonal belief formation is more properly based on testimony and epistemic dependence, especially in the sciences (Hardwig, 1985, 1991; Kukla, 2012). Social epistemology recognizes that the social dimensions involved in acquiring, processing, distributing and assessing knowledge ought to be represented in epistemological theorizing (Goldberg, 2020, p. 417). Particularly in scientific inquiry, the practice of belief formation cannot be not reduced to the intuition-belief-fact relationship, which social processes are key to claims of objectivity, reliability, and empirical success (Longino, 1990; Goldman, 1999; Solomon, 2001). Yet, how do we understand belief formation processes involved in generating knowledge? The actual empirical practices of inference, justification, and criticism are surprisingly under investigated, considering the centrality of the objective for philosophy.

Rather than focusing solely on the beliefs of individuals, it is more useful to investigate interpersonal practices leading to reliable belief-formation along three aspects (Goldberg, 2020, pp. 420–422): First, considering epistemic dependency, we recognize that “our epistemic tasks are often socially distributed among the members of our epistemic community” (420). Second, related to epistemic dependency are our expectations which “reflect various norms [explicit agreements, norms as part of established practice, tacit or implicit norms] that regulate our interaction with other epistemic agents” (421). The third, concerns the relation between epistemic dependence and epistemic expectations. According to Goldberg, this relation is the basis for our belief in other epistemically dependent individuals: “To the extent that one's epistemic environment bears on the proper assessment of one's beliefs, we will need to rethink the nature of epistemic assessment itself, in a way that reflects the various epistemic dependencies and social norms that are implicated in the production and sustainment of belief” (Goldberg, 2020, p. 422). We argue that these objectives can be addressed better with qualitative methods for philosophical analysis.

In contrast with interpersonal epistemology, collective epistemology investigates how judgments aggregate to justified belief within a group (List, 2005) and the relation between the reliability of justification processed by an individual compared to a collective (Goldberg, 2014, pp. 11–12). This distinction between interpersonal and collective practice is important for reframing the sort of “rational reconstruction” at which empirical philosophy of science aims. When researchers communicate their experimental results in scientific papers, they do not communicate an exact, unmediated, real-time report of their research experience about the uncertainties, errors, and challenges they met over the course of months or years (Schickore & Hangel, 2019; Medawar, [1963] 1996). It is not uncommon to hear utterances similar to “We don't know how the results were obtained. The postdoc who did all the work has since left to start a bakery” or “Blood samples were spun at 1500 ​rpm because the centrifuge made a scary noise at higher speeds” (Bezuidenhout, 2015, p. 1510). In peer-reviewed articles, we may learn nothing about the actual justificatory processes and the deliberations among the collaborators within the workgroup that led up to the results. What we do get instead, is a hyper-idealized reconstruction of scientific results communicated and embedded in arguments and reasons that is a “critical reconstruction of the descriptive scientific thinking process” that has actually happened (Schickore, 2008).

To avoid such flights from the empirical world, qualitative approaches allow us to study in detail processes of reasoning as relevant for the formation or development of collective belief, judgments, decisions, and justification. With qualitative methods, philosophers can retain focus on the interpersonal processes of reasoning as our objective. Many social epistemologists today believe that “conceptual analysis will not get us very far in identifying the conditions of effective knowledge generation on a societal scale” (Collin, 2020, p. 21). They turn instead to adopting empirical approaches by drawing on disciplines like cognitive science, sociology, anthropology and economics for their findings without adapting these methods themselves for their most pressing questions (Collin, 2020, p. 21). Rather than evaluate whether science satisfies a certain set of scientific values we assume are in operation, we should investigate empirically how epistemic values are enacted in scientific practice. Therefore, we advocate for adapting conceptual analysis from a normative to a descriptive endeavor.

2.3. Sociology of science & social reasoning

Sociologists of science (as well as social psychologists) have studied extensively the social practices that occupy many philosophers of science, such as collaboration, co-authorship, scientific progress, and reward systems (Hall, Vogel, & Croyle, 2019; Katz & Martin, 1997; Laudel, 2002; Nosek, Spies, & Motyl, 2012). Sociology in the tradition of Robert Merton was rather amenable to philosophers, arguing for the insulation of science from political influence (Merton, 1938). Yet, philosophers for a long time have turned to sociologists more for their findings and theories than their methods. Interchange during the 1980s became less amiable when sociologists like Knorr-Cetina (1981) and Latour and Woolgar (1986) began investigating scientists’ activities within the labs using a range of ethnographic methods that treated science as a mere cultural activity and motivated skepticism about the rational basis of experimental practice.

Somewhat orthogonal to philosophers, if not completely opposed, the interest of sociology of science was directed from the start at the practical, institutional, and cultural aspects of science. For instance, in the early 1980's Harry Collins (1983) investigated how social conditions in science and society influence scientific development and how they impact science by influencing controversies, such as how the prestige of some research programs can influence what is considered as factual (see also Maasen et al., 2012, 50). Unlike “weak” sociology that takes “truth” for granted, Collins argued along with David Bloor, Barry Barnes, and others for a “strong program” in the sociology of scientific knowledge that treated both “true” and “false” scientific theories the same. For the strong program, what becomes “scientific truth” depends not only on observation, experimentation, epistemic social processes, trust in expertise, and competence, but also on the reputation of scientific intuitions, personal interests, and informal coalitions (see Maasen et al., 2012 92–93).

Pickering (1992) revoked the conceptual split between experimental practices and cognitive theorizing by including all scientific activities into practices, from testing and circulating scientific theories, to creating models of scientific entities and demonstrating them to peers (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016). Sociologists also challenged the binary categories of art and science by arguing that scientists transformed “laboratory reasoning” into “literary reasoning” when communicating results in publications (Knorr-Cetina, 1981; Latour & Woolgar, 1986). Latour (1987) is well-known for provocative characterizations of science as a cultural enterprise in which scientists are an interest group bent on rhetorical persuasion more than understanding the nature of reality. The ensuing interchange of the 1990s between “science defenders” (including philosophers attacking “cultural relativism”) and “science studies” (including sociologists studying science as a cultural practice) was dubbed the “Science Wars” (Gieryn, 1999; Zammito, 2004). Much of this interchange was combative attacks on “postmodernist myths about science” (e.g., Koertge, 1998), though other philosophers had more productive discussions, particularly about gender, social processes, and values in science (Douglas, 2009; Longino, 2002; Solomon, 2001). Yet, philosophy of science was methodologically ill equipped to respond adequately to many of the core challenges from sociology involving the social constructivism of scientific knowledge.

Rather than deepening this disconnect, empirical philosophy of science aims to build on the shared interests across sociology and philosophy. While sociologists focus on the individual as “the embodiment of the collectivity, or set of collectivities” (Collins, 2019, p. 11), the organization of epistemological exchange between individuals is what interests philosophers. However, there are many overlapping concerns. Philosophers of science from Popper to Longino and their sociological peers have emphasized practices of peer criticism ever since Merton (1973 [1942]). In his methodological textbook, Harry Collins (2019) discusses how both sociologists and philosophers of science face similar challenges and have similar goals, sometimes orthogonal to those of the practicing scientists they observe. While scientists might be immersed in tacit knowledge and therefore not keen to explain every detail of their knowledge-making processes, they still need to justify, reason, and deliberate about their scientific activities in order to communicate to other peers within and outside of their research environments. Therefore, descriptions, judgements and (meta)reflections are the conceptual parts of scientific actions.

With his sociology of scientific methods Collins leads the way for empirically oriented philosophers using qualitative methods to investigate the two sides of the same coin: concepts and actions in scientific practice (Collins & Evans, 2007). Deeper understanding is certainly a philosophical goal (if not “the” goal), and so philosophers would be remiss to ignore the extant sociological approaches to studying meaning within scientific fields particularly when systematically studying scientific inquiry of working scientists by using qualitative methods.

2.4. Qualitative empirical philosophy of science and its successes

While empirical approaches in philosophy of science began with historical case studies, philosophers have now begun to explore using qualitative methods to inform philosophical questions (see Wagenknecht et al., 2015).

In an early articulation of empirical philosophy of science, Nancy Nersessian contends that philosophers can shift “focus from the products to the processes, from the structures to the practices” of science by using empirical methods from sociology, history, and cognitive science (Nersessian, 2008, p. 5). Nersessian has advocated for using empirical methods like psychological experimentation, computational modeling, and historical case-studies including qualitative methods like ethnography, to develop and test philosophical hypotheses. Her “cognitive-historical method” triangulates between different empirical methods to construct “thick descriptions” of “the nature of the interaction of reasoning and representation in conceptual innovation and change” (Nersessian, 2008, pp. 9–10; but see also Nersessian, 2022).

In philosophy, current research adapting sociological methods elicits how experimental scientists make use of collaborative practices depending on their research experience, social position within the group, and their experimental role, e.g., as modeler or experimenter (see MacLeod & Nersessian, 2016). Qualitative data can provide in-depth descriptions for understanding how scientists conceptualize and describe their experiences and how their justifications are scrutinized mutually in their research teams. When MacLeod and Nersessian (2016) conducted ethnographic studies of model-building practices in systems biology and bioengineering sciences, they showed how a qualitative analysis of problem-solving practices is essential for understanding methodological choices, the influence of background knowledge, and practices of collaboration and integration within these fields. Over a five-year period, their team collected qualitative data using ethnographic methods of qualitative interviews and participant observation during lab meetings. With these, they found that interdisciplinary teams engage in “adaptive problem solving” as a response to the complexity of problems in systems biology as well as the lack of disciplinary constraints. While previously philosophers have argued for the desirability of interdisciplinary research (Longino, 2012) (for reasons related to pluralism and incommensurability), this empirical qualitative philosophy was able to describe actual features of the processes of interpersonal reasoning involved by collecting empirical data. It thereby contributes to closing the gap between philosophical theorizing and scientific inquiry in practice (e.g., Nersessian, 2022; Nersessian & MacLeod, 2022).

According to Osbeck and Nersessian (2015, p. 14), what makes empirical philosophy of science empirical is how it is “rooted in the instrument and cannot be understood apart from it,” noting that the instrument includes researchers themselves. They divide empirical approaches into three types (historical, qualitative, and experimental/quantitative) emphasizing the complementarity of different methods. As Osbeck et al. (2010) illustrate, social, personal and cognitive processes are intricately woven together in scientific thinking. For instance, Osbeck and Nersessian (2011) investigated biomedical engineering researchers’ accounts of their problem solving practices, underscoring the need to consider emotional expression to be intimately and importantly tied to the cognitive achievements and social negotiations of laboratory practices. Likewise, Wagenknecht (2016) conducted qualitative research in two research groups over the course of two years, and she was able to explain the roles of epistemic trust and dependence in collaborative research when generating knowledge.

These empirical approaches can inform existing philosophical debates. Some non-empirically working philosophers have argued that collectives have the primary epistemic power in collaborative research and, thus, that scientific knowledge is thus irreducibly social (Longino, 1990; Solomon, 2001). Yet, others have critically studied the benefits and challenges of collaborative research including the accountability, motivations, and aggregation of group belief (Rolin, 2015; Wray, 2006, 2007). To analyze theories of social epistemology, e.g., concerning epistemic dependence and trust in collaborative research settings empirically, Wagenknecht (2016) builds on Hardwig (1991) and others, utilized qualitative methods and found that the joint commitment approach to collective knowledge does not apply as well as the epistemic dependence approach to collaboratively created scientific knowledge. The collectivity of scientific knowledge would depend on mutual epistemic dependence which “prevents individual scientists from providing a scientific justification for the piece of knowledge in question” (Wagenknecht, 2016, p. 169). Qualitative inquiry can also contribute to understanding how scientists describe the relationship between themselves as individual researchers and as part of a group when accumulating and justifying knowledge (Hangel & Schickore, 2017). Empirical studies such as these do not abandon epistemological questions but instead rearticulate them for non-idealized subjects by localizing and contextualizing justification and epistemic acceptability, thus recognizing the interdependence of cognitive agents when generating knowledge and the plurality of that knowledge (see Longino, 2002).

Other philosophers of science have successfully used qualitative data analysis (QDA) to understand how values influence science. For instance, to better understand the impacts of conflicts of interest (COIs), Holman and Geislar (2018) used grounded theory to analyze the transcript of a public “Voice of the Patient” meeting at the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). They coded testimony from different speakers of their experiences with an illness and constructed different themes with diverging narratives for those with and without COIs. Because the FDA listened more to the narratives of patients with COIs (that suggested the need for a new drug), Holman and Giesler concluded that any policy for managing bias needs to take measures to avoid becoming co-opted by industry.

While not completely different, philosophers use qualitative methods in a manner that is nonetheless different in part from how they are used by sociologists. Our use of qualitative methods is related to the original sociological use; the difference refers to the adaptation according to the different research questions and research objectives. Sociologists have studied knowledge production in science as socially determined, and they are typically interested in belief-forming practices concerning their dynamics (Laudel, 2002), studying how convictions and beliefs come about, sensitive to the context of those beliefs, their cultural situatedness, behavior, or relational norms (Knorr-Cetina, 2002). Instead, qualitative empirical philosophy of science profits from Helen Longino's approach whose theory is programmatic for arguing against the dichotomy between social and rational processes of belief formation. We are not claiming that knowledge is just social (as did the strong program in sociology), but rather that the “social dimension of cognitive processes and the interaction of social and cognitive factors give us a more accurate understanding to assess scientific judgments” (Longino, 2002, p. 206). We anticipate a full-blown use of this approach to retain keen interest in the content of research practice, specific dynamics, and the quality of scientific reasoning.

In regards to social epistemology, we share with those like (Goldman, 2004) an interest in reliable belief-formation in interpersonal and collective practice, by focusing on evaluating these practices. Yet to reach this aim, we contend that conceptual analysis can be complemented fruitfully with less abstract and less idealized models. To accurately describe scientists' evaluations, meta-reflection and interactions among each other, one must have access to the relevant normatively effective knowledge practices in their many-fold dimensionality. The heterogeneity of actual scientific practice as described throughout this section suggests that conceptual analysis is complementary but by itself not sufficient to address the many factors involved in scientific practice. By adding qualitative approaches to our methodological toolkit, we can lay out the network and interdependencies of how scientific and social values and norms interact in practice. We then can analyze scientists’ conceptions of doing science and display relevant network relations in the context of their role in generating knowledge. Scientific knowledge in the making needs scientists sharing concepts in addition to engaging in actions in order to reach the common aim to generate knowledge within the collectivity that is science. Thus, qualitative empirical philosophy of science is well posed to understanding more deeply the interpersonal processes of epistemic and social reasoning. By adapting empirical methods for philosophy, through interviews, participant observation, and other qualitative methods, we can get to the core of scientific reasoning to inform social epistemology empirically.

3. Qualitative methods and methodology

In contrast to quantitative methods involving numbers and statistics, with qualitative methods we collect data such as descriptions of experiences and social processes as well as conceptualizations about scientific practice, e.g., descriptions of experimenting or (meta)reflections about doing science. While quantitative data analysis can contribute valuable insights for philosophical theorizing by providing evidence for hypotheses and explanations of patterns, qualitative analysis can discover how processes follow patterns relational to their context. Qualitative analysis involves participant observation, collaborative coding of interviews and other texts, refinement of emerging patterns, and external auditing, which together provide intersubjective rigor and accountability to the process of conceptualization (Osbeck & Nersessian, 2015). In this section, we describe how to adapt and utilize qualitative methods for empirical philosophy, especially how to investigate philosophically relevant aspects of interpersonal processes of social reasoning in science. We discuss qualitative methods in terms of research questions, data collection, and data analysis. We point to the standards in qualitative sociology and recommend that interested philosophers train in the methods to inform their philosophical questions (Bryman, 2015; Bursten et al., 2016; Collins, 2019; Hammersley & Atkinson, 2007; Maxwell, 2012; Yin, 2016).

Qualitative methods are particularly well suited to answering questions about social processes which is crucial because philosophy has traditionally focused more on individual agency. Qualitative empirical approaches allow philosophers to inform a variety of research questions involving scientific reasoning, practice, and judgment in collaborative and collective settings. For instance, how do scientists themselves conceptualize and describe situations of choice and shared decision-making? How do they manage standardized procedures versus vague, non-algorithmic decisions in empirical inquiry? How do working scientists develop confidence in their reasoning and confidence in their and shared decisions, such as whether empirical evidence is sufficiently strong to support a particular hypothesis? What sorts of criteria do they identify, and how do different members of a research team contribute to evaluation and revision? Who raises concerns about criteria and uncertainties, and how do other members respond? How do they negotiate the indeterminacy of questions, criteria, and results? How do scientists understand the presence of value judgments in their work, if at all? Do they reflect on the relationships between epistemic and non-epistemic criteria for decision-making, and if so, how?

To answer such questions, one must collect qualitative data. Common methods of qualitative data collection include but are not limited to: conducting structured surveys and qualitative interviews, videotaping observations, collecting “jottings” and field notes, and immersing the qualitative investigators in a given context (participant observation). To meet the standards of qualitative research, researchers triangulate emerging patterns by using different methods of primary and secondary data (including text documents).

Data collection must be well targeted, particularly qualitative data that can be very dense and nuanced. For instance, suppose the goal is explicating interpersonal, collaborative, and collective knowledge generation to investigate epistemic aspects of scientific practice, with qualitative methods. Then we need to access how scientists themselves reflect on, describe, and communicate processes of their knowledge building activities. We can collect scientists’ reasons, justifications, decisions, and (meta-)reflections about their research activities by combining a variety of qualitative methods. After selecting a particular field suitable for investigation, we suggest conducting problem-centered expert interviews. This approach merges the theory-generating expert interview that discusses the social relevance of expert knowledge and the problem-centered interview that offers a dialogic-discursive interview procedure investigating individual perspectives (Döringer, 2021).

To triangulate the data, ethnographic fieldwork, e.g., participant observation in research group meetings, would contribute to embedding the qualitative working philosopher into the specific scientific collectivity (Tuana, 2013). In addition, philosophers can invite scientists to reflect on their reasoning collectively, e.g., by drawing tree graphs about their organizational knowledge, which will give detailed insights about processes involved in scientific practice from the stance of working scientists (see Jehn & Jonsen, 2010). In addition, we can collect experimental notebooks, research protocols, group meeting minutes, publication drafts, e-mail correspondence, and reviews, and triangulate these with the data collected previously. All these methods of qualitative data collection taken together can be analyzed systematically to reveal epistemologically significant practice at various levels of social interaction when generating knowledge.

Following qualitative data acquisition comes qualitative data analysis. Qualitative researchers use a variety of methodologies for analysis, including grounded theory, constant comparison method, discourse analysis, phenomenological psychology, narrative research, content analysis, and more (Charmaz, 2014; Dixon-Woods, Agarwal, Jones, Young, & Sutton, 2005; Wertz et al., 2011). These methodologies enable qualitative researchers to identify recurring themes and patterns in how language is used and to what effect (Dixon-Woods et al., 2005). These methods value both written and conversational texts, considering everything that promotes the process of understanding through iteration between the interview, the contexts of the texts, the analysis of them, and thus the whole dataset produced by the research (Maxwell, 2012). “All the products of analysis—descriptions, interpretations, and theories—have their reference to the textual expressions of another person to which they continually refer. This qualitative principle of emergent knowledge is a basic and unifying one that assures that the findings of qualitative research have an evidentiary base” (Wertz et al., 2011, p. 331). Depending on the research question, different approaches and combinations of methods will prove more or less useful, so they have to be chosen with care and intention as discussed in detail by Rosile, Boje, Carlon, Downs, and Saylors (2013).

QDA is the ideal method to show how social processes are related to changes in experimental practice and scientific judgment (Maxwell, 2012). It allows the analyst to gain perspectives on practices, beliefs, habits of mind, and interactions that would otherwise be invisible from the perspective of those not present at the sites of inquiry. By analyzing how values are enacted in practice we can contrast norms as they are actually practiced with the more idealized normative justifications given by practitioners and then develop normative guidance that helps us to do science in specific contexts of investigation (Wimsatt, 2007, pp. 5–9). Rather than conflating social and epistemic aspects of science, QDA facilitates parsing the different norms and factors operative in science. For instance, qualitative researchers have been able to tease out social practices in interdisciplinary problem solving, involving cognitive flexibility, interactional expertise, and epistemic awareness (Nersessian, 2019). In addition to transparently revealing effective knowledge practices, with qualitative methods we can describe the networks and interdependencies of how these values and norms interact in practice (see Section 4.1).

Although knowledge generation is one of the core targets of philosophical inquiry, so far it remains an open question how scientists' justificatory processes in actual scientific practice are connected to the rational reconstructions in publications. To better understand these justificatory processes and find satisfactory explanations, we need to assess how judgments of individual scientists and their individual inferences interact in collaborative settings to produce knowledge. Similarly, to better understand what encapsulates the practitioners’ relevant practice of evaluating and credentialing, we need to better understand how scientists defend, adjust, and develop relevant standards as part of generating knowledge. In short, to understand the epistemologically significant part of scientific practice, we can utilize qualitative methods to analyze how social and epistemic values are embedded in actual practice and how they relate and contribute to generating knowledge.

Now, one might wonder: should philosophers even be in the business of answering empirical questions about social processes, which has traditionally been the domain of sociology? Does this not change the very nature of what counts as a genuinely philosophical research question? We will discuss this further in section 5, but we will begin by laying out two responses here. First, this concern assumes a strict demarcation between philosophy and sociology as well as a strict division of labor between the “theorists” and “fact finders.” These assumptions pigeonhole practitioners of each discipline to their “proper” ends of the abstract/concrete spectrum of research questions, neglecting the possibilities of research in the middle and the benefits of boundary crossing. Second, the concern itself should be understood as a restrictive form of boundary-work because it delegitimizes the credibility of philosophers innovating outside their traditional bounds and presupposes a static methodology for philosophy. Particularly for philosophers of science interested in scientific practice, we suggest there are significant benefits for asking and answering research questions with empirical components, the subject of our next section.

4. Benefits and limitations of qualitative empirical philosophy of science

We have argued in the previous section that qualitative methods have prospects for philosophical use, particularly for those interested in interpersonal and collective reasoning processes in science (though there are certainly other applications). Sharing, evaluating, and enforcing epistemic norms are social processes and at the same time part of epistemic scientific practices. Thus, we contend that with qualitative methods we can analyze scientists’ reasoning and thereby add to social epistemology of present science. This contrasts with philosophers who have rejected more psychological and sociological approaches to understanding science in its historically contingent, value-laden state. Nonetheless, there is no perfect methodology for the study of scientific practice, only more or less fitting ways to answer questions of interest with evidential support. Qualitative methods are one of many empirical approaches, but they can provide much in-depth understanding about relevant processes.

In the remainder of this section, we discuss benefits and limitations of utilizing qualitative methods in philosophy of science. First, we discuss how empirical methods can help answer some of the classic philosophical questions about normativity in science. Second, we argue that, while qualitative methods cannot provide complete universality, they do enable more sense of the scope of a study's actual generalizability.

4.1. The sort of normativity empirical methods do (and do not) provide

Past philosophers of science have spurned sociological findings as inconsequential to the (narrowly defined) epistemic tasks of philosophy. According to Reichenbach (1938), philosophers have three tasks: a descriptive task of rationally reconstructing the thinking process of scientists, a critical task of revealing the logical flaws in such (idealized) reasoning, and an advisory task of recommending new frameworks and conventions (Schickore, 2008). On this traditional positivist view, normativity is understood as what scientists epistemically ought to do grounded in logical justification. This stance however is overly idealized because it does not account for the contingent but relevant psychological, social, and historical factors that actually play into scientific reasoning (Solomon, 2001), nor does it account for knowledge as concurrently social and cognitive (Longino, 2002).

If we do not engage with the de facto empirical grounding of scientific arguments, philosophy of science is in danger of constructing highly idealized approaches with little direct use to or impact on experimentally working scientists. If philosophy of science wants to keep those normative roles of criticizing and advising—not at the expense of all idealization but by staying closer to scientific reasoning of collaborating scientists—it has to target both descriptive and normative forms of scientific reasoning. To arrive at normative claims, qualitative working philosophers can start by describing and analyzing the various normative factors emerging from the scientists' claims, values, and descriptions of processes in the collected data, and by relating them to the social aspects that contribute (e.g., epistemic dependence and trust) but also can be detrimental (e.g., confirmation bias, publication bias) to these processes. Our aim is to include “descriptions of participants' evaluations (and meta-evaluations) of knowledge practices …, thereby aiming to incorporate a ‘normative’ dimension …” such that our “normative assessment” relies on “the very knowledge practices that characterize the epistemic community itself” (Goldberg, 2020, p. 417). Thus, the normative power of the proposed approach lies in analyzing different modes of normativity embedded in actual scientific practice.

In contrast to the traditional approach, we contend that describing social practices of how socially enacted epistemic norms and social norms (e.g., power relations) are embedded is key for understanding science philosophically. We can learn how scientists describe and conceptualize their practices of evaluating reliability (e.g., through independent confirmation and replication) and the implicit and explicit values they use in their assessment (e.g., shared communal norms, standards of evidence). A qualitative approach to philosophy of science starts with this descriptive aspect of scientific practice, with special attention to how practices relate to the various normative aspects of generating knowledge collaboratively. To give an illustrative example: Hangel and Schickore (2017) used qualitative interviews to examine how working scientists themselves understand, conceptualize, apply, and communicate norms and standards for “good research practice.” They described the goals of researchers, such as the desire for replication and career advancement, and the tensions between them. After gaining an in-depth understanding of the actual processes driving collaborative knowledge generation as described by working scientists, these findings can update existing theories and thereby advance philosophical understanding.

While we cannot arrive at fundamental first principle normativity (deducted from theory), qualitative methods can nevertheless deliver a less idealized, more accurate description of the use and effect of normativity embedded in actual scientific practice. For instance, Andersen and Wagenknecht (2013) describe how epistemic trust in interdisciplinary research groups with conflicting background knowledge can be established: Assigning a person responsible for interdisciplinary integration who has “interactional expertise in all areas covered by the group” enables collaborating researchers to learn about and trust each other's background assumptions. It also encourages exchange to negotiate background assumptions between collaborators from different fields. However, besides contributory expertise in an area of expertise and interactional expertise in a field of the collaborator, the “shared intention to produce a joint result strongly constrains individual contributors.” This means subscribing to a joint claim requires that individual contributions need tight consultation and agreement of other team members (Andersen & Wagenknecht, 2013, 1895–96).

What does this “embedded normativity” look like? Normativity is an obvious component of science if science is understood as a social practice (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016, pp. 456–57): “Because communal practices are collaborative, organized, and goal oriented, they have a normative structure, at both the individual and the group level.” We can identify different normative structures that contribute to the interdependencies of social and epistemic norms for generating knowledge. The normative structure is visible in the methodological rules for reliable research; however, in practice, they need to be enacted by individual scientists through their interpersonal interactions, e.g., through rule following behavior, enforcing group norms, expecting certain social interactions. To hold each other accountable to the epistemic process of social rules, scientists ascribe to epistemic aims, such as generating reliable, reproducible knowledge claims. These processes can be better assessed by qualitative empirical philosophy of science by investigating: how shared rules of accountability among working scientists are addressed, enforced, and critically evaluated, both in and between research groups. Unfortunately, power relations and destructive forms of competition can impede epistemic processes both within research groups and in peer-review processes (Hangel & Schickore, 2017, Hangel & Schmidt-Pfister, 2017).

As Hicks and Stapleford (2016, p. 457) also note, normativity and accountability are closely related, for “the boundaries of communal practice are established sociologically and politically.” This allows us to distinguish between various norms, such as the norms of a field, the norms within a goal-oriented project, the norms of accountability among collaborators and between socially interacting individuals, and the norms of accountability to one's peers and non-peers. All of these norms are involved in epistemic decisions about appropriating techniques and methods for producing reliable and meaningful knowledge. They must come together for evaluating and credentialing epistemic practice, as well as for communicating the scope and reliability of results while holding each other mutually accountable. “[T]he core revelation of the practice turn, of the turn from identifying science as disembodied knowledge to seeing science as an activity, was the recognition that all the features of that activity …, instrument, experimental techniques, … cognitive habits, self-professed [and communal] goals were not context for science; they constituted science in a given time and place” (Hicks & Stapleford, 2016, p. 472). These sorts of embedded normativity from theory can be made visible with scientists contextualized descriptions of their own activities by using qualitative methods.

By distinguishing different normative aspects constitutive of scientific practices, we can relate them to Longino's “modalities of knowledge.” The undertaking surpasses prior projects of sociology of science and traditional philosophy of science, adapting the sociological methods of the former for the epistemic goals of the latter. Programmatically, Longino identifies three modalities of knowledge: agents of knowledge, content of knowledge, and practices warranting knowledge. Concerning actual scientific practice, “[c]ognitive agents are interdependent, and their knowing is a social status, [and] not a kind of psychological state” (Longino, 2002, p. 204). Longino describes epistemic acceptability in terms of procedural norms, as including critical scrutiny from diverse perspectives, “uptake of criticism, public standards, and tempered equality of intellectual authority” (Longino, 2002, p. 135). While this has been criticized as overtaxing the scientific community (Solomon, 1994), if we apply this model not to the scientific community at large but to the collaborative working group, the benefit becomes clear: Individual agency of knowledge becomes the (interpersonal) interdependence of cognitive agency that contributes to knowledge. Justificatory processes of credentialing include discursive interactions among researchers, criticisms from different perspectives, and examinations and evaluations of descriptive and normative implications that are actual practice in every collaboration, experimental or otherwise. By reclaiming social practice as the object of philosophical inquiry, Longino emphasizes knowledge as concurrently social and cognitive (Longino, 2002, p. 204), and we argue that with qualitative methods we can utilize her theory for empirically grounded philosophical analysis.

Solomon's (1994) social empiricism presents an alternative theory about the role of values in science that would benefit from qualitative methods. Any relevant influencing factors, which she calls vectors—internal or external, epistemic or social—can be coded/signified using qualitative methods, related to each other in “Network Relations” using QDA software to visually display and understand how they interplay and influence collaborative decision making and evaluate for normative appropriateness. With qualitative methods we gain a means to evaluate theories like Solomon's, evaluate how social and epistemic norms are deliberated transparently, and whether power relations influence epistemic systems.

We argue that our discipline would benefit from relating epistemic justification to cognitive agency beyond the individual agent by including intersubjectivity between agents in the content of knowledge via procedural analysis. Instead of foreclosing empirical aspects of cognitive concepts, philosophical evaluations of normative epistemological concerns should open up to an “account of scientific knowledge that features contextuality of justification, interdependence of cognitive agents, and (possible) plurality of content” (Longino, 2002, p. 96). Precisely because interpersonal interactions and knowledge production can be analyzed well with qualitative methods, empirical philosophical investigators can access social dimensions of cognition like normativity for the correction of epistemically undermining factors. Adapting qualitative methods will advance philosophical methods for social epistemology of science: “The empirical question is how belief, commitment, or theory and hypothesis acceptance are stabilized in the face of the openness of inquiry. The normative question is how they are stabilized in a non-arbitrary way that has probative value” (Longino, 2002, p. 205). Both the descriptive and the normative aspects of philosophy of science stay intact but can be bridged by the evaluative possibilities through qualitative analysis by explaining how descriptive and normative aspects of scientific reasoning contribute to generating knowledge. With QDA we can on the one hand distinguish between processes with references (descriptions/justifications) which ought to be used for a shared epistemic aim (reliable knowledge) and, on the other hand, processes with evaluative character, where scientists inquire/revise/share their norms and how they followed them, descriptively. In scientific inquiry, epistemic results can be produced well or poorly and thus are being evaluated following norms and justifications.

This approach to normativity is more empirically adequate, along with being more socially and historically contextualized. The findings from one investigated research field, say cognitive psychology, might not be directly applicable for e.g., medical science, if they have different embedded normativity. Nonetheless, most complex problems nowadays warrant interdisciplinary effort and qualitative methods are particularly well equipped to support understanding between concepts and actions in scientific practice, even if the conceptions need interdisciplinary harmonization. Limiting the explanatory power of qualitative findings to the scope and context of their application raises legitimate questions about methodological generalizability, particularly in contrast to traditional philosophical methods aiming for universality.

4.2. The extent of generalizability, Not “If…” But “How Much?”

A major methodological worry in philosophy of science using historical case studies is the limited ability to generalize from one particular case to a more universal phenomenon. There are two interrelated concerns here, about sample size and bias: first, if philosophy of science uses too small a number of cases, we will not have a sufficient basis for generalizing about science; second, the methodology of case studies does not safeguard against bias, either in the selection of a case, emphasis of certain features, or interpretations about lessons learned (Steel, Gonnerman, & O’Rourke, 2017). While most philosophers have avoided conversations about methodology (but see Schickore, 2017), discussions on the limits of historical case studies have abounded in &HPS (e.g., Kinzel, 2015; Mauskopf & Schmaltz, 2012; Nickles, 1986). Joe Pitt (2001) framed the core issue as a dilemma for historicist philosophers: On the one hand, if they choose a case study that confirms their philosophical theory, then they might have cherry-picked from the set of possible examples or, worse yet, manipulated the data to fit the theory (intentionally or not). The influence of our pre-theoretical assumptions on the choice of case studies could undermine their ability to support philosophical claims about the nature of science. On the other hand, if philosophers begin with case studies to reduce bias in their methodological judgments, they are unable to determine how generalizable the example is beyond the specific case and context. If nothing else, these risks of sample bias and hasty generalization ought to motivate caution for philosophers, given our supposed goal of universal understanding: “As philosophers we seek universals, but the only universal regarding science is change. … The lesson to be learned is that if philosophers wish to use historical cases to bolster their positions, then we will have to use very long studies and we will have to figure out how to relate the history to the philosophical point without begging the question” (Pitt, 2001, p. 374).

Some philosophers have responded to Pitt's dilemma by arguing that we ought to strategically group case studies, either longitudinally over time or comparatively across space, to account for context and to understand scientific change: “Case studies cannot and should not be expected to yield universal methodologies or epistemologies. Rather, they yield local or, better, regional standards—and fallible ones at that” (Burian, 2001, p. 400). The key shift here for philosophers of science was to sacrifice the traditional philosophical aim of “universalizing methodologies or epistemologies” or (pejoratively) “pseudo generality,” which they found to be untenable for philosophy of science, in place of more concrete historically informed studies (Burian, 2001, p. 401).

Rather than understanding history as particular and philosophy as general, some advocates of &HPS take the relation to be spanning the continuum between concrete episodes in the history of science and abstract ideas in philosophy: “the episode is a concrete instantiation of the general concepts (the characters, the setting, the type of events to be expected, etc.), and each episode also contributes to the articulation of the general concepts” (Chang, 2012, p. 111). This iterative approach between history and philosophy lends particular strength to the use of in-depth historical case studies to expose the limited scope of existing philosophy (ChoGlueck, 2021). Yet, even if we are willing to sacrifice universality (i.e., complete generality), how can we determine the degree of generality of our philosophical understanding? As Hasok Chang (2012, p. 111) formulates it, “An abstraction becomes general only when it has been applied widely. Successful application functions as confirmation, but without the presumption of universality in what is confirmed.”

We contend that, in contrast to historical case studies, qualitative methods have the benefit of a more informed understanding of the degree of generality achieved by an individual empirically informed study. The clearest benefit comes in terms of sampling. Larger samples increase internal variation, and random samples reduce selection bias. For most historical case studies, the sample size is often a single example (n ​= ​1). Some longitudinal or comparative studies have a larger sample size and thus capture more variation (or at least better establish an in-depth description). The key questions regarding the generality of the sample surround its representativeness: does this sample serve as a representation of a large group or phenomenon? Which groups and in what context? A sample of an entire lab's researchers is certainly representative of that lab but less of the group's larger institution, academic discipline, and national character. Thus, if one wants to generalize to any of these larger groups, one would need to sample more strategically across them (see Maxwell, 2012).

Qualitative methodology helps researchers align their methods with their conclusions, such that they can iteratively adjust one to fit the other. A crucial consideration is reflection on the limits of one's methods, including sample bias, researcher bias, and the reactivity of participants. Qualitative researchers have developed a variety of strategies for handling their validity threats, such as having intensive, long-term engagements with participants; collecting a rich, detailed set of different types of data; actively searching for negative cases and disconfirming evidence; assessing “saturation” of data; and triangulating patterns across different types of data (Bryman, 2015; Maxwell, 2012).

While none of these problems provides guaranteed generality (or solves the basic problem of induction), they do enable empirical philosophers to make a more informed and confident assessment of the extent of the generality of their results. For instance, to understand the different epistemic attitudes toward soft condensed matter physics in biological systems, philosopher Darrell Rowbottom conducted 10 unstructured interviews with scientists in three different departments, and engaged in a variety of observations of meetings, lectures, and workshops. He noted that his study was not so much conclusive as suggestive: “The sample size was relatively small, so the result should be taken to frame tentative hypotheses which are worthy of subsequent quantitative study … At the very least, my findings illustrate interesting differences that sometimes occur at the aforementioned interface; and considering these is worthwhile even if they are not as widespread as I believe—by way of a somewhat bold hypothesis—that they are.” (Rowbottom, 2011, p. 145). The limitations are clear, as well as the means of surmounting them: with a follow-up study of a larger sample across a larger number of institutions, the support for this hypothesis would be bolstered.

Consequently, because of their ability to mitigate selection and emphasis biases, we ought to think of sociological methods as complementary to historical case studies (Steel et al., 2017). In contrast to qualitative methods, more quantitative methods like large surveys provide even more generality. Sociologists doing “mixed-methods research” often combine qualitative methods with quantitative ones to provide both breadth and depth with their work. There are trade-offs to increasing one's sample size, not just in terms of cost and convenience, but also in more epistemic terms: while quantitative methods can provide strong understanding of correlative and causative relationships between variables, often qualitative methods provide a richer understanding of the social processes of individuals manifesting those relationships. For instance, while a quantitative bibliometric study might show correlations between gender and citation, a qualitative study involving interviews and observations could actually explain the social processes responsible for such correlation, e.g., the impacts of barriers to accessing funding and of experiences of discrimination on network building capacity.

More formal mathematical methods currently in use by philosophers, such as agent-based modeling (ABM), also provide insights into abstract relationships and mechanisms. Like other forms of empirical testing, ABMs give some degree of confirmation/disconfirmation of theories, such as providing evidence for philosophical hypotheses and explanation of social patterns in science (Frey & Šešelja, 2018). Several philosophers have already experimented with combining historical and quantitative methods (e.g., Frey & Šešelja, 2018; Holman & Bruner, 2017). Similarly, quantitative insights can be fruitfully augmented by more detailed qualitative studies of the practices that create such correlations. Thus, particularly in terms of concrete, causal knowledge, qualitative methods can provide strong grounds for philosophical understanding.

5. Conclusion

We have argued for expanding the methodology in philosophy of science to normalize the use of qualitative methods by philosophers. We have provided reasons for adapting qualitative methods for use in philosophy and elsewhere and for developing new empirical approaches. There has been some limited change in philosophical inquiry over the past few decades, both in philosophy of science to more historical methods and in social epistemology toward quantitative methods. Yet, despite its considerable successes, qualitative empirical philosophy remains at the fringes of our discipline. This suggests that methodological debates are not free of political and social factors; therefore, the question of pursuitworthiness is also a partially political and sociological question. Our argument for the legitimacy and credibility of qualitative methods in philosophy depends on the reasons we have given, though we would not be surprised to encounter more disciplinary resistance based on more traditional ideas about philosophical practice and the somewhat charged relationship to (constructivist) sociology.

In particular, we foresee disciplinary anxieties about what makes empirical philosophy of science (especially with qualitative methodologies) distinct from sociology of science. For instance, one might wonder if there really is a major difference between a philosopher of science using sociological methods to inform their philosophical work in an interdisciplinary way and a philosopher of science using qualitative methods drawn from sociology that are now regarded as properly philosophical methods.2 Can we expand our methodology without having qualitative empirical philosophy of science collapse into traditional sociology of science? Would it not be better to simply do interdisciplinary research with both philosophers and sociologists working together on a team and bringing different toolkits?

While we do advocate for interdisciplinary approaches, we think this concern is misplaced. First, when philosophers utilize qualitative methods, their usage differs significantly from sociologists, both in style and substance. For instance, sociologists like Collins (2019) focus on the dynamics of collectives in generating knowledge, such as how socialization into scientific communities is a disciplinary process for individuals to become members of new epistemic cultures. In contrast, when philosophers like MacLeod and Nersessian (2016) studied interdisciplinary research teams, they focused on how these diverse groups of modelers and biologists adapt their reasoning to the absence of shared disciplinary problems and norms and instead develop their alternative practices of problem solving. Second, we perceive no strict dichotomy between sociological findings and philosophical ones, but rather a continuum, as reasoning itself is a social practice dependent on communal norms. Furthermore, empirical philosophy of science can enage more directly with the sociological results generated by observations of laboratory and field studies as practiced by a sociology of scientific knowledge (Collins, 2019) as well as the emerging field of the “Science of Science” (see Fortunato et al., 2018). By combining empirical methods with traditional approaches like rational reconstruction and conceptual analysis, philosophers can better understand how scientists themselves handle epistemic, ethical, and other philosophical challenges in their research.

Specifically, in collaborative research, scientists’ abilities to communicate clear accounts of the criteria, norms, and standards used to validate empirical evidence are becoming crucially important for the functioning of science itself (MacLeod & Nersessian, 2016). As collaborators in interdisciplinary contexts, scientists need conceptual tools to express, explain, justify, critique, and evaluate their methodological standards and engage in a meta-discourse about epistemic and social values in research to different audiences. To communicate the scope of their results as well as their own accountability, researchers often reflect on the potential tensions between social and cognitive/methodological criteria for decisions under uncertainty (Schickore & Hangel, 2019; Nersessian, 2019). Integrating how scientists understand and conceptualize evaluative criteria in the face of intrinsic indeterminateness is thus an important part of understanding how science really works and, possibly, how it might be improved.

As philosophers of science, we are trained in methodological criticism and are very quick to pick out the faults and problems in specific methods. While this is useful in understanding the limits of any approach, it can lead to an unachievable search for “the perfect method.” All methods, however, are merely tools, more or less fit for answering certain questions, more or less adaptable for different contexts. As philosophers, we need to acknowledge the limitations of the methodology we traditionally use, including conceptual, historical, and the benefits of new alternatives. We advocate for more openness to qualitative methods to help us achieve and expand our philosophical goals. Accordingly, we do not suggest abandoning our traditional conceptual methods (at least not altogether). By investigating the actual processes of scientific reasoning such as justification, evaluation, and credentialing, we envision complementarity between our philosophical objectives and empirical approaches.
