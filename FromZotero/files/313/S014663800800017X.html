Organic Geochemistry

Volume 39, Issue 8, August 2008, Pages 1210-1214

TLC–FID (Iatroscan) analysis of heavy oil and tar sand samples

Author links open overlay panel, Steve R. Larter, ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.orggeochem.2008.01.013Get rights and content

Abstract

TLC–FID (Iatroscan) reproducibility of the fractionation [saturated hydrocarbons, aromatic hydrocarbons, resins, asphaltenes (SARA)] of heavy oil and tar sands samples has been optimized by avoiding overloading and channeling caused by very small sample spot sizes at the origin of the silica - coated rods (S-III). Doping the rods with copper (II) sulfate increased the signal response for the saturate and aromatic fractions and resulted in a decrease in the relative standard deviation of the measured peak areas for all four SARA fractions. Comparison of precipitated asphaltenes and column chromatographic separation of the maltene fraction with Iatroscan data is problematic because resins are typically occluded in the precipitated asphaltenes and polar fractions appear to have variable flame ionization detector (FID) response factors.

1. Introduction

TLC–FID (thin layer chromatography–flame ionization detector in an Iatroscan instrument) has long been used as a rapid and inexpensive way to determine SARA fractions (saturate + aromatic = hydrocarbon, resin (or NSO) + asphaltene = polar) in crude oils and bitumen extracts (Karlsen and Larter, 1991, Bharati et al., 1994, Bharati et al., 1997). However, the analysis of heavy oils and tar sands (HOTS) poses a number of additional problems. HOTS have relatively high amounts of polar compounds that are retained near the spot location of the TLC rods, potentially causing both separation and quantitation problems. Additionally, the polar fractions have variable quantities of heteroatoms, resulting in different FID response factors, further complicating the quantitation of the SARA fractions.

Two operational variables have been investigated and optimized with respect to improving the reproducibility of the Iatroscan data generated for HOTS samples. In addition to internal reproducibility, these operational parameters were also investigated with respect to obtaining SARA results that were more directly comparable with fraction concentrations determined from asphaltene precipitation (usually with cold n-pentane) followed by open column chromatography using sequential elution of the maltene fraction from a silica gel/alumina column.

2. Method and results

A sample of tar sand bitumen used as an internal laboratory standard was analysed repeatedly over a period of several months with standard Si-III rods and Si-III rods that had been doped with copper (II) sulfate (Kaimal and Shantha, 1984, Kaimal and Shantha, 1986, Shantha, 1992). Asphaltene precipitation and column chromatography of the sample yielded 12.4 wt% saturates, 36.9 wt% aromatics, 13.6 wt% resins (or NSOs) and 37.1 wt% asphaltenes. These gravimetric results were determined in the organic geochemistry laboratory of the Calgary office of the Geological Survey of Canada and no rigorous analytical error analysis is available for them. The analytical method used was to precipitate the asphaltene fraction with about 30 volumes of cold n-pentane (C5), followed by open column chromatography on an activated silica gel:alumina (1:3) column sequentially eluted with n-C5, 50:50 n-C5:dichloromethane (DCM), methanol and chloroform. The final chloroform eluate was presumed to be asphaltenes and its mass was added to the precipitated asphaltene fraction. A constant sample loading of 1:100 (wt:wt) was used and consistent solvent volumes were adjusted to the weight of the adsorbent used (3.5, 4, 4, and 4 ml of solvent, respectively, per g of adsorbent). A mass balance recovery of the fractions afforded 97.4% of the original sample.

The samples (1 μl of a 15 mg bitumen/ml DCM solution) were loaded on to the rods using a commercial device (SES 3202 IS03 Sample Spotter) at two injection speeds, resulting in two spot sizes (1–1.5 and 3.5–4 mm). The rods were sequentially developed using hexane (∼ 90% of rod length), hexane:toluene (1:1 v:v, ∼ 60%), and DCM:methanol (93:7 v:v, ∼ 30%) and then analysed using an MK-6 Iatroscan. The resolution between the resin and asphaltene fractions was lower and less reproducible for the small spot size samples relative to the larger spots derived from the rapid injection of sample on to the rod (Fig. 1).

Means and standard deviations were calculated for peak areas for each of the four SARA fractions of the tar sand sample for 350 replicate analyses on standard Si-III rods and 30 replicates were run on the Cu-doped rods. The statistical summary of the results is shown in Table 1.

3. Discussion

3.1. Spot size

Counterintuitively, it was observed that a very small spot (1–1.5 mm) applied using a slow, pulsed injection with the automated spotting device did not provide for better separation of the SARA fractions (first four traces in Fig. 1). Better separation and more reproducible integration results were obtained by using a relatively rapid injection, resulting in an initial spot size of 3.5–4 mm. This result may stem from the fact that SARA fractionation is not a continuous chromatographic process but rather a separation based on developing the rods with a series of different polarity solvents to different distances and thereby carrying different polarity groups of the sample up the rod near the various solvent fronts. The apparent loss of resolution with the very small spot size may result from overloading of the silica at the spot location and commensurate channeling of the solvents through the spot. The variability of the FID response could also be due to differences in ionization efficiency resulting from slight variations of the concentration of material in the adsorbent phase. The better separation of the resins and asphaltenes with the larger spot size provided a more reproducible minimum signal between the peaks and this allowed the electronic integration to yield more reproducible integration areas.

3.2. Cu-doped rods

The second variable tested was the properties of the silica rods after doping them with copper (II) sulfate (Kaimal and Shantha, 1984, Kaimal and Shantha, 1986, Shantha, 1992). The effect was an alteration in the absolute response of the different hydrocarbon fractions (Table 1), especially the saturate compounds (increased by a factor of ∼ 3.3×) and aromatic compounds (increased by ∼ 2.4×). The response factor of the resin fraction decreased by a small amount on the Cu-doped rods (∼ 0.8×) and the asphaltene fraction response factor increased slightly (∼ 1.2×) (Fig. 2). Thus the overall peak areas of the polar compounds were reduced, while those of the hydrocarbons increased. It also appears that doping the rods with copper not only alters the FID response factor, but also reduces the variability in all fraction peak areas (Table 1).

Iatroscan SARA analysis of individual fractions obtained from asphaltene precipitation and open column chromatography demonstrates that the two analytical techniques do not provide precisely the same separation (Fig. 3). This is not surprising because the normal procedure is to use ∼ 30 volumes of cold n-C5 to precipitate the asphaltene fraction, followed by a separation of the maltene fraction on a silica gel/alumina column, whereas the Iatroscan approach uses silica (S-III rods) as the adsorbent and sequential solvent thin layer chromatography separation. The separation of fractions is thus operational and relies on the effective polarity of the solvents and adsorbents. Additionally, the asphaltene precipitate occludes material that belongs in the maltene fraction, mainly resins, and in order to obtain a “clean” asphaltene fraction it is necessary to repeat the precipitation at least one or two more times (Karlsen and Larter, 1991). Because this repeated precipitation is time consuming, multiple asphaltene precipitations are not normally carried out unless the asphaltene fraction is specifically being isolated for subsequent analysis. Iatroscan analysis of resin and asphaltene fractions that were separated using a single precipitation and column chromatography (Fig. 2) suggests that the TLC approach provides an effective separation of these fractions (Karlsen and Larter, 1991).

3.3. Comparison of gravimetric and Iatroscan SARA analysis

Direct comparison of SARA data generated by the two techniques is problematic not only because of the occlusion of resins within the asphaltene precipitate, but also because the FID response factor inherent in the Iatroscan approach is not an issue in the gravimetric determination of fractions from column chromatography (Fig. 2). The response factor of the resin and asphaltene fractions varies as a function of the loading on the TLC rods (unpublished results), and also appears to vary with the specific chemistry of the fractions (Karlsen and Larter, 1991). The non-linear response of the FID signal for the resin and asphaltene peaks reflects different ionization efficiency at different fraction densities on the rods. The non-linearity of response is somewhat reduced by the use of Cu treated rods, but the separation of the resin and asphaltene fractions using the thin layer and column chromatography approaches is still significantly different. As a result, it is necessary to have a calibration standard that is chemically similar to the samples being analysed. Despite this limitation, Iatroscan may well provide a higher degree of reproducibility for the amounts of polar fractions than column chromatography in the case of HOTS samples. In part, this reduced scatter may result from reduced oxidation during the TLC fractionation, reduction or elimination of physical occlusion during a single precipitation using cold n-C5, and also because evaporation of the solvent from a partially volatile sample is more consistent during development and analysis of the TLC rods than the rotary evaporation or nitrogen blow down of solvent from the precipitated or eluted fractions obtained with the gravimetric approach.

4. Conclusions

TLC–FID (Iatroscan) separation and detection of SARA fractions of heavy oil and tar sand (HOTS) samples is degraded by loading the sample in a very small spot at the origin of the rod. It appears that this causes overloading of the adsorbent on the TLC rod, channeling of the solvents used for development and commensurate loss of resolution of the resin and asphaltene fractions.

The use of Cu-doped Si-III rods increases the response of the FID to the saturate (∼ 3.3×) and aromatic (∼ 2.4×) fractions and yields an improvement in the reproducibility of the peak areas for all four SARA fractions.

Iatroscan analysis of HOTS samples provides a better separation of resin and asphaltene fractions than a single, cold n-C5 precipitation followed by column chromatography. Variable response factors for the polar fractions indicate that absolute calibration of the TLC–FID peak areas will be problematic.
